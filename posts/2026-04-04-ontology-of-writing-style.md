# An Ontology of Writing Style: A Framework for Understanding How We Write

Most writing advice focuses on rules: use active voice, cut adverbs, vary sentence length. These are useful, but they miss the bigger picture. Before you can apply a rule, you need to understand *what kind of writer you are trying to be* — and what kind of writing the moment calls for.

That's what an ontology of writing style is for.

An ontology doesn't tell you what's good or bad. It maps a domain — identifies the entities, their properties, and the relationships between them. Applied to writing style, it gives you a shared vocabulary for talking about how texts work, and a framework for making intentional choices rather than default ones.

---

## What Is "Writing Style"?

Before building a taxonomy, we need to define the thing we're taxonomizing.

Writing style is the *how* of writing, distinct from the *what*. Two writers can cover the same topic — say, the causes of World War I — and produce texts that feel completely different: one dense and academic, the other propulsive and journalistic; one ironic and essayistic, the other sober and documentary.

The facts don't determine the style. The writer does.

Style operates across at least five distinct dimensions:

1. **Voice** — who is speaking, and what personality comes through
2. **Tone** — the emotional register and attitude toward subject and reader
3. **Structure** — how ideas are organized and sequenced
4. **Diction** — word-level choices: register, specificity, connotation
5. **Rhythm** — sentence-level patterns: length, syntax, cadence

An ontology of writing style maps these dimensions and the values they can take — and shows how different combinations produce recognizably different styles.

---

## Dimension 1: Voice

Voice is the personality that comes through in text. It's what makes you recognize a David Foster Wallace essay in the first paragraph, or know you're reading Hemingway before you check the byline.

### Voice: Authorial Presence

| Value | Description | Example Markers |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Effaced** | Writer disappears; prose feels impersonal | Passive constructions, no first-person, formal hedges |
| **Institutional** | Voice of an organization, not an individual | "We" as corporate, third-person self-reference |
| **Personal** | Individual personality comes through clearly | First-person, opinions stated, idiosyncrasies present |
| **Performative** | Voice itself is part of the content | Meta-commentary, self-awareness, style as meaning |

### Voice: Authority Register

| Value | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Expert** | Speaks from specialized knowledge; assumes reader is catching up |
| **Peer** | Speaks alongside the reader; assumes shared frame |
| **Curious** | Speaks from genuine inquiry; explores rather than pronounces |
| **Entertainer** | Speaks to engage; expertise subordinated to experience |

These aren't ranked. A curious voice can be more illuminating than an expert one. An entertainer can teach more than a peer. But mistaking one for another — trying to entertain when readers want expertise, or pronouncing when they want companionship — is where writers lose their audience.

## Dimension 2: Tone

If voice is *who is speaking*, tone is *how they feel about what they're saying* — and about the reader.

Tone is often described as a simple spectrum from formal to informal, but that's too flat. Tone has multiple axes:

### Tone Axes

**Formality**: `Casual ←————————→ Formal`

Formality governs register: vocabulary choices, contraction usage, sentence complexity, whether you address the reader as "you" or "one."

**Emotional Temperature**: `Detached ←————————→ Passionate`

This axis captures how much feeling the writer brings. Academic writing traditionally aims for the detached end; personal essays often run hot. Neither is inherently better — the question is fit.

**Attitude Toward Subject**: `Reverent ←————————→ Irreverent`

Does the writer treat the subject with gravity or with play? This axis is independent of formality: you can be formally irreverent (think: dry satire) or informally reverent (think: fan writing about something genuinely loved).

**Attitude Toward Reader**: `Challenging ←————————→ Reassuring`

Some writing assumes the reader is sharp and pushes them; other writing meets the reader where they are and guides gently. Academic writing often challenges; how-to writing often reassures.

Tone failure usually happens when one axis is miscalibrated for the context. A formally written but emotionally cold piece on a topic that deserves warmth feels alienating. A casual, reassuring piece on a complex topic that demands challenge feels condescending.

## Dimension 3: Structure

Structure is how the ideas are organized in time — the sequence a reader follows from first sentence to last.

### Top-Level Structural Patterns

**Deductive (Top-Down)**
Claim first, evidence follows. The reader knows where they're going from the start; the rest of the piece justifies, elaborates, or defends the opening claim. Most journalism and business writing is deductive.

**Inductive (Bottom-Up)**
Evidence first, claim emerges. The reader is taken through the reasoning before the conclusion is revealed. Academic argumentation and mystery narratives share this structure.

**Narrative**
Events in time (not necessarily chronological). Conflict → complication → resolution. The claim, if any, is embedded in the story rather than stated directly.

**Exploratory**
The structure follows the writer's thinking rather than a predetermined shape. Essays and intellectual blogs often use this mode — the reader experiences inquiry rather than conclusion.

**Modular**
Independent sections that can be read in any order. Reference documentation, encyclopedias, and listicles are modular. Each unit is self-contained.

### Micro-Structure: The Paragraph

Paragraphs also have structural choices:

- **Topic sentence first**: Classical expository structure; each paragraph announces its content
- **Topic sentence last**: Suspense structure; the paragraph builds to its point
- **No topic sentence**: Impressionistic; coherence is felt rather than stated

## Dimension 4: Diction

Diction is word-level style: which words the writer chooses and why.

### Register

| Register | Characteristics | Context |
|----------|----------------|---------|
| **Technical** | Domain-specific vocabulary, precision over accessibility | Specialist-to-specialist communication |
| **Academic** | Latinate vocabulary, hedging language, citation culture | Scholarly discourse |
| **Journalistic** | Plain language, concrete nouns, active verbs | General audiences, speed of consumption |
| **Conversational** | Contractions, colloquialisms, spoken cadences | Informal contexts, personal connection |
| **Literary** | Deliberate word choices, connotation-heavy, often elevated | Fiction, essays, writing where language is itself an object of attention |

### Specificity

A key diction choice is the level of specificity: the difference between "a fast car" and "a 1967 Shelby GT500 doing ninety on an empty highway at dawn."

Specificity creates vividness, authority, and credibility. Abstraction creates generality, transferability, and breadth. Most writing benefits from moving between the two — anchoring abstractions in specific examples, then zooming out to draw the general point.

### Connotation Management

Words don't just denote; they connote. "Thin," "slender," "gaunt," "emaciated," and "svelte" have roughly similar denotations but carry very different feeling-tones. Skilled writers choose words for their connotative weight, not just their dictionary meaning.

## Dimension 5: Rhythm

Rhythm is the music of prose — the patterns of length, stress, and pause that give writing its feel when read aloud (or subvocalized while reading silently).

### Sentence Length Patterns

**Uniform short**: Creates urgency, clarity, sometimes monotony. Hemingway famously exploited this. It can feel muscular or can feel breathless, depending on context.

**Uniform long**: Creates complexity, subordination, a sense that ideas are densely connected. Henry James. Sometimes illuminating, sometimes suffocating.

**Varied**: Most effective for sustained reading. Long sentences build complexity; short sentences land conclusions. The rhythm of alternation controls reader attention and emphasis.

### Syntactic Choices

**Hypotaxis** (subordination): Clauses nested within clauses, ideas placed in explicit logical relationship. "Although the weather was poor, and despite the late hour, they pressed on because the alternative was unthinkable."

**Parataxis** (coordination): Ideas placed side by side without explicit logical connectives. "The weather was poor. It was late. They pressed on." The reader infers the relationship.

Hypotactic prose feels analytical, hierarchical, complex. Paratactic prose feels immediate, cinematic, suggestive.

## Putting It Together: Style Profiles

Individual dimensions combine into recognizable style profiles. Here are a few canonical examples:

### The Academic Paper
- **Voice**: Effaced, Expert
- **Tone**: Formal, Detached, Reverent (of evidence), Challenging
- **Structure**: Deductive or Inductive, Topic-sentence-first paragraphs
- **Diction**: Academic register, High specificity, Careful connotation management
- **Rhythm**: Long sentences, Hypotactic

### The Tech Blog Post
- **Voice**: Personal, Peer
- **Tone**: Casual-to-formal, Medium temperature, Often irreverent, Reassuring
- **Structure**: Deductive, Modular sections
- **Diction**: Mixed technical/conversational, High specificity
- **Rhythm**: Varied, with short punchy sentences for emphasis

### The Literary Essay
- **Voice**: Personal, Curious
- **Tone**: Variable (often ironic or reflective), High temperature
- **Structure**: Exploratory, No consistent topic sentences
- **Diction**: Literary register, Moves between abstract and specific
- **Rhythm**: Deliberately varied, Parataxis and hypotaxis in tension

### The Journalistic Feature
- **Voice**: Effaced or Personal (depending on outlet), Expert or Peer
- **Tone**: Formal-to-casual (depending on outlet), Controlled temperature, Relatively reverent
- **Structure**: Narrative or Deductive (inverted pyramid), Strong topic sentences
- **Diction**: Journalistic register, Very high specificity, Controlled connotation
- **Rhythm**: Varied but generally shorter, Parataxis predominates

## Why This Matters

An ontology of writing style isn't just descriptive — it's generative and diagnostic.

**For writers**, it provides a decision framework. Instead of defaulting to whatever style you absorbed in school, you can consciously ask: what voice is right for this piece? What tone does this reader need? What structure serves this argument? The taxonomy makes implicit choices explicit and therefore makeable.

**For editors**, it provides a diagnostic vocabulary. Instead of "this doesn't quite work," you can say "the voice is too effaced for a topic that needs personal authority" or "the structure is exploratory but the reader needs deductive — they need to know where they're going." Precise diagnosis leads to precise revision.

**For readers and critics**, it provides an analytical vocabulary. Close reading becomes more tractable when you can name what you're responding to. "I don't like this" is a dead end; "this piece uses academic diction in an exploratory structure and the mismatch creates friction" is a starting point.

**For AI systems** trained on text, a writing style ontology provides a structured interface to what would otherwise be a diffuse set of properties. Instead of "write like a blog post," a system (or a human) can specify: Personal voice, Peer authority, Deductive structure, Journalistic diction, Varied rhythm. The ontology makes style composable and parameterizable.

## Limitations and Open Questions

No ontology is complete, and this one has real limitations:

**Cultural context**: Writing style is culturally situated. What counts as "formal" in one tradition may read as "cold" in another. The dimensions here are weighted toward Western, Anglo-American print traditions.

**Genre**: Genre creates expectations that modulate all five dimensions. A legal brief written in "casual conversational" style isn't just stylistically unusual — it's professionally invalid. Genre norms aren't in this ontology.

**Interaction effects**: The dimensions interact in complex ways. A very high-specificity diction in an exploratory structure can feel meandering; the same specificity in a deductive structure feels rigorous. The ontology maps dimensions but doesn't fully capture their interactions.

**Evolution**: Styles evolve. "Formal" in 1850 and "formal" in 2026 are different things. An ontology is a snapshot, not a permanent map.

These limitations don't make the ontology useless — they make it honest. A map that acknowledges where it's incomplete is more useful than one that claims to cover everything.

---

## Conclusion

Writing style isn't a single thing you have or don't have. It's a space of choices across multiple dimensions — voice, tone, structure, diction, rhythm — and every piece of writing occupies a particular position in that space, intentionally or not.

The goal of an ontology isn't to prescribe a correct position. It's to make the space legible so that writers can navigate it deliberately.

Most style failures aren't failures of skill. They're failures of intention — the writer defaulted to a style without asking whether it was the right style for the job. The ontology is a tool for asking that question rigorously, and for having the vocabulary to answer it.

Write for the moment. Know which dials you're turning and why.
